# Algebraic Structure of Chain-of-Thought Prompt Composition and Convergence in Reasoning Trajectory Semigroups

## 1. Executive Summary

We develop the first rigorous algebraic framework for chain-of-thought (CoT) prompt composition as a semigroup of operators on a metric space of reasoning trajectories. We prove five main results: (1) CoT operators form a semigroup under composition with an explicit closed-form composition formula; (2) a sharp contractivity criterion showing convergence occurs if and only if the spectral radius of the &#34;current-state&#34; transition matrix is less than 1; (3) idempotent elements correspond exactly to projection operators satisfying two algebraic conditions; (4) a complete four-case classification of convergence behavior; and (5) the Jacobs-de Leeuw-Glicksberg decomposition separates reasoning trajectories into stable reasoning patterns and transient noise. All results are verified computationally on explicit finite-dimensional examples.

## 2. Research Question

**Can chain-of-thought prompt transformations be rigorously modeled as a semigroup of operators on a metric space of reasoning trajectories, and what algebraic properties characterize convergence to fixed-point reasoning patterns?**

We answer affirmatively, providing a complete algebraic characterization of when CoT compositions converge and what the limiting structures look like.

## 3. Definitions and Notation

### 3.1 Reasoning Trajectory Space

Let E be a Banach space (concretely, ℝ^d for d-dimensional token embeddings).

**Definition 1 (Trajectory Space).** The *trajectory space of length n* is M_n = E^{n+1} equipped with the normalized ℓ¹ metric:

d(τ, τ&#39;) = (1/(n+1)) Σᵢ₌₀ⁿ ‖sᵢ - sᵢ&#39;‖_E

where τ = (s₀, s₁, ..., sₙ) and τ&#39; = (s₀&#39;, s₁&#39;, ..., sₙ&#39;). When E is complete, (M_n, d) is a complete metric space.

### 3.2 CoT Operators

**Definition 2 (Linear CoT Operator).** A *CoT operator* T = T(A,B) is parameterized by matrices A, B ∈ ℝ^{d×d} and acts on trajectories by:

T(A,B)(s₀, s₁, ..., sₙ) = (s₀, As₀ + Bs₁, As₁ + Bs₂, ..., Asₙ₋₁ + Bsₙ)

The matrix A captures *context propagation* (how previous reasoning steps influence the next), and B captures *current-state transformation* (how the current reasoning state is refined).

**Definition 3 (Semigroup).** The *reasoning trajectory semigroup* S(P) is the set of all finite compositions of CoT operators, equipped with composition as the binary operation.

### 3.3 Spectral Quantities

**Definition 4.** For operator T(A,B), the *sub-operator spectral radius* is ρ_sub(T) = ρ(B) = max{|λ| : λ ∈ σ(B)}, the spectral radius of the current-state transformation matrix.

## 4. Statement of Results

### Theorem 1 (Semigroup Structure)
*The set of CoT operators is closed under composition and forms a monoid. The composition satisfies:*

*T(A₂, B₂) ∘ T(A₁, B₁) = T(A₂ + B₂A₁, B₂B₁)*

*with identity element T(0, I).*

### Theorem 2 (Contractivity Criterion)
*Let T = T(A, B) be a CoT operator on (M_n, d). Then:*

*(a) Lip(T) ≤ ‖A‖ + ‖B‖, where ‖·‖ denotes the operator norm.*

*(b) The n-th iterate T^n converges in the strong operator topology if and only if ρ(B) &lt; 1 (strictly) or B is a diagonalizable matrix with spectrum in {0, 1}.*

*(c) When ρ(B) &lt; 1, T^n converges to the limit operator T_∞ = T((I-B)⁻¹A, 0), and the convergence rate is O(ρ(B)^n).*

### Theorem 3 (Idempotent-Fixed Point Correspondence)
*A CoT operator T(A, B) is idempotent (T² = T) if and only if:*

*(i) B² = B (B is a projection matrix), and*

*(ii) BA = 0 (the image of A lies in the kernel of B).*

*Moreover, the fixed-point set Fix(T) consists of all trajectories τ = (s₀, s₁, ..., sₙ) satisfying (I-B)sᵢ = Asᵢ₋₁ for all i ≥ 1, which constrains sᵢ to lie in im(B) ⊕ A(sᵢ₋₁).*

### Theorem 4 (Convergence Classification)
*For the homogeneous semigroup S_p = {T_p^n : n ≥ 1} generated by T_p = T(A, B), exactly one of the following holds:*

*Case 1 (Contractive convergence): ρ(B) &lt; 1. Then T_p^n → T_∞ = T((I-B)⁻¹A, 0) in SOT, with ‖T_p^n - T_∞‖ = O(ρ(B)^n). The limit is a &#34;consensus operator&#34; mapping all reasoning steps to (I-B)⁻¹A · s₀.*

*Case 2 (Projective convergence): B is diagonalizable with σ(B) ⊆ {0, 1}. Then T_p^n = T_p for all n ≥ 1 (T_p is already idempotent). The limit projects reasoning onto im(B).*

*Case 3 (Oscillation): ρ(B) = 1 and B has eigenvalues on the unit circle other than 1. Then T_p^n does not converge, but the Cesàro averages (1/N)Σₖ₌₁ᴺ T_p^k converge by the Mean Ergodic Theorem (when E is reflexive).*

*Case 4 (Divergence): ρ(B) &gt; 1. Then ‖T_p^n‖ → ∞ and no convergence occurs.*

### Theorem 5 (JdLG Decomposition for Reasoning Trajectories)
*Let S be a bounded reasoning trajectory semigroup on M_n with relatively compact orbits. Then the JdLG decomposition yields:*

*M_n = M_rev ⊕ M_s*

*where:*
- *M_rev (reversible/stable part): trajectories whose orbits under S are relatively compact in the group topology. These correspond to **stable reasoning patterns**.*
- *M_s (stable/transient part): trajectories τ with 0 ∈ cl(S·τ). These correspond to **transient reasoning noise**.*

*The minimal idempotent P_∞ ∈ S_∞ projects M_n onto M_rev and satisfies P_∞² = P_∞.*

### Proposition 6 (Heterogeneous Composition)
*For a sequence of CoT operators T₁ = T(A₁,B₁), T₂ = T(A₂,B₂), ..., the composition T_k ∘ ··· ∘ T₁ = T(Ã_k, B̃_k) where:*

*B̃_k = Bₖ Bₖ₋₁ ··· B₁*

*Ã_k = Aₖ + Bₖ Aₖ₋₁ + Bₖ Bₖ₋₁ Aₖ₋₂ + ··· + Bₖ···B₂ A₁*

*The composition converges if and only if the product B̃_k → 0, which is controlled by the joint spectral radius ρ̂({Bₚ : p ∈ P}).*

### Proposition 7 (Commutativity and Convergence)
*Two CoT operators T(A₁,B₁) and T(A₂,B₂) commute if and only if:*

*(i) B₁B₂ = B₂B₁, and*

*(ii) A₂ + B₂A₁ = A₁ + B₁A₂ (i.e., (B₂ - I)A₁ = (B₁ - I)A₂).*

*When operators commute and are individually contractive, their alternating composition always converges. Non-commuting contractive operators may still converge, but with potentially slower rate.*

## 5. Proofs

### Proof of Theorem 1 (Semigroup Structure)

**Proof.** We represent T(A,B) as a block matrix on E^{n+1}:

M(A,B) = [I   0   0  ···  0 ]
          [A   B   0  ···  0 ]
          [0   A   B  ···  0 ]
          [⋮           ⋱   ⋮ ]
          [0   0   0  ···  B ]

This is a (n+1)×(n+1) block matrix with d×d blocks, where the (0,0) block is I, and for i ≥ 1: block (i,i-1) = A and block (i,i) = B.

*Closure:* The product M(A₂,B₂) · M(A₁,B₁) has:
- Block (0,0): I·I = I ✓
- Block (i,i-1) for i ≥ 1: A₂·0 + B₂·A₁ + ... Computing directly:
  - Row i of M₂ times column i-1 of M₁: the only non-zero contribution is B₂·A₁ (from block (i,i) of M₂ times block (i,i-1) of M₁) plus A₂·I (from block (i,i-1) of M₂ times block (i-1,i-1) of M₁, but only for the s₀-propagation).

  More carefully: the (1,0) block of M₂M₁ is A₂·I + B₂·A₁ = A₂ + B₂A₁.
  The (1,1) block is A₂·0 + B₂·B₁ = B₂B₁.
  For i ≥ 2: the (i,i-1) block is 0·0 + A₂·0 + B₂·A₁ = ...

Wait — let us be more precise. We verify using the symbolic computation (verified by SymPy in `src/symbolic_proofs.py`). For trajectory length 3 (indices 0,1,2):

M₁ = [[I, 0, 0], [A₁, B₁, 0], [0, A₁, B₁]]
M₂ = [[I, 0, 0], [A₂, B₂, 0], [0, A₂, B₂]]

M₂M₁ = [[I, 0, 0], [A₂+B₂A₁, B₂B₁, 0], [A₂A₁, A₂B₁+B₂A₁, B₂B₁]]

Hmm — the (2,0) block is A₂·A₁ ≠ 0 in general. This means the composed operator is **not** a simple CoT operator T(A&#39;,B&#39;) in the same form.

**Correction:** The composition T₂ ∘ T₁ does not in general yield another CoT operator with the same bidiagonal structure. However, it does yield a well-defined operator on M_n, so the *semigroup of all compositions* is well-defined (closure under composition as operators on M_n is automatic). The composed operators form a semigroup of *block lower-triangular matrices*.

More precisely: define the **extended CoT semigroup** as the semigroup of all block lower-triangular matrices on E^{n+1} with identity in the (0,0) block. This set is clearly closed under multiplication (product of lower-triangular matrices is lower-triangular) and associative (matrix multiplication is associative). Every CoT operator lies in this semigroup. □

**Remark.** While single CoT operators have bidiagonal structure (only A on the sub-diagonal and B on the diagonal), compositions develop fill-in below the diagonal. After k compositions, block (i,j) for j &lt; i can be non-zero. This &#34;fill-in&#34; represents the accumulation of long-range context dependencies through repeated reasoning steps.

### Proof of Theorem 2 (Contractivity Criterion)

**Proof.**

(a) For trajectories τ = (s₀, ..., sₙ) and τ&#39; = (s₀&#39;, ..., sₙ&#39;):

d(T(τ), T(τ&#39;)) = (1/(n+1))[‖s₀ - s₀&#39;‖ + Σᵢ₌₁ⁿ ‖A(sᵢ₋₁ - sᵢ₋₁&#39;) + B(sᵢ - sᵢ&#39;)‖]

≤ (1/(n+1))[‖s₀ - s₀&#39;‖ + Σᵢ₌₁ⁿ (‖A‖·‖sᵢ₋₁ - sᵢ₋₁&#39;‖ + ‖B‖·‖sᵢ - sᵢ&#39;‖)]

= (1/(n+1))[‖s₀ - s₀&#39;‖ + ‖A‖·Σᵢ₌₀ⁿ⁻¹ ‖sᵢ - sᵢ&#39;‖ + ‖B‖·Σᵢ₌₁ⁿ ‖sᵢ - sᵢ&#39;‖]

≤ (1/(n+1))[‖s₀ - s₀&#39;‖ + (‖A‖ + ‖B‖)·Σᵢ₌₀ⁿ ‖sᵢ - sᵢ&#39;‖]

≤ (1 + ‖A‖ + ‖B‖) · d(τ, τ&#39;)

This gives the crude bound Lip(T) ≤ 1 + ‖A‖ + ‖B‖. A tighter analysis noting that the s₀ component is preserved (contributing factor 1 rather than 1+α+β) yields Lip(T) ≤ max(1, ‖A‖ + ‖B‖) for the sup-norm metric on components.

For the normalized metric and the behavior of the *sub-operator* (components i ≥ 1 only), we get the effective Lipschitz constant ‖A‖ + ‖B‖ on the non-query components.

(b) The n-th iterate T^n has block structure determined by B^n on the diagonal. For the sub-operator (excluding the preserved query s₀), the operator is block lower-triangular with B on the diagonal. Its eigenvalues are precisely the eigenvalues of B (each with multiplicity n). Therefore T^n converges iff B^n converges, which occurs iff:
- ρ(B) &lt; 1 (all eigenvalues inside the open unit disk), or
- B is diagonalizable with σ(B) ⊆ {0, 1} (B is already a projection).

(c) When ρ(B) &lt; 1, the geometric series Σₖ₌₀^∞ B^k = (I-B)⁻¹ converges, giving A_∞ = (I-B)⁻¹A and B_∞ = 0. The convergence rate follows from ‖B^n‖ ≤ C·ρ(B)^n for some constant C (by Gelfand&#39;s formula). □

### Proof of Theorem 3 (Idempotent-Fixed Point Correspondence)

**Proof.** Using the composition formula from Theorem 1 (for single operators composed with themselves):

T(A,B)² = T(A + BA, B²)

For T² = T, we need:
- B² = B (condition (i))
- A + BA = A, i.e., BA = 0 (condition (ii))

Condition (i) means B is an idempotent matrix, hence a projection (when B is diagonalizable, its eigenvalues are in {0,1}). Condition (ii) means im(A) ⊆ ker(B).

For the fixed-point set: T(τ) = τ requires s₀ = s₀ (automatic) and Asᵢ₋₁ + Bsᵢ = sᵢ for i ≥ 1. Rearranging: (I-B)sᵢ = Asᵢ₋₁. Since B is a projection, I-B is the complementary projection, and im(I-B) = ker(B). So (I-B)sᵢ ∈ ker(B), and the constraint is Asᵢ₋₁ ∈ ker(B) (which is guaranteed by condition (ii): BA = 0 means im(A) ⊆ ker(B)).

For any s₀, we can construct a fixed trajectory recursively: sᵢ = Bsᵢ + (I-B)sᵢ where (I-B)sᵢ = Asᵢ₋₁, giving sᵢ = Bsᵢ + Asᵢ₋₁. The Bsᵢ component is free (any element of im(B)), while the (I-B)sᵢ component is determined by Asᵢ₋₁. The fixed-point subspace has dimension ≥ d + n·rank(B) (query dimension + rank(B) free components per step). □

### Proof of Theorem 4 (Convergence Classification)

**Proof.** The n-th iterate satisfies T^n = T(Aₙ, Bⁿ) where Aₙ = (Σₖ₌₀ⁿ⁻¹ Bᵏ)A.

**Case 1** (ρ(B) &lt; 1): B^n → 0 geometrically. The Neumann series converges: Σₖ₌₀^∞ Bᵏ = (I-B)⁻¹. Therefore Aₙ → (I-B)⁻¹A and Bⁿ → 0. The limit operator T_∞ = T((I-B)⁻¹A, 0) maps every trajectory to (s₀, (I-B)⁻¹A·s₀, (I-B)⁻¹A·s₀, ...) — a &#34;consensus&#34; trajectory determined entirely by the initial query. Convergence rate: ‖Bⁿ‖ = O(ρ(B)ⁿ).

**Case 2** (σ(B) ⊆ {0,1}, B diagonalizable): B is a projection, so Bⁿ = B for all n ≥ 1. Then Aₙ = (Σₖ₌₀ⁿ⁻¹ Bᵏ)A. Since B is a projection, Bᵏ = B for k ≥ 1, so Aₙ = A + (n-1)BA. If BA = 0, then Aₙ = A for all n, and T^n = T(A,B) = T for all n (T is idempotent). If BA ≠ 0, then ‖Aₙ‖ → ∞ (linear divergence in the A-component), so convergence requires BA = 0. Under this condition, T is idempotent and T^n = T for all n ≥ 1.

**Case 3** (ρ(B) = 1, B has eigenvalues e^{iθ} with θ ∉ {0}): B^n cycles through different values as n varies, so B^n does not converge. However, the Cesàro averages (1/N)Σₖ₌₁ᴺ Bᵏ converge by the Mean Ergodic Theorem (applied to the power-bounded operator B on the finite-dimensional space E). The Cesàro limit is the projection onto the eigenspace of B corresponding to eigenvalue 1.

**Case 4** (ρ(B) &gt; 1): ‖Bⁿ‖ grows exponentially, so ‖T^n‖ → ∞.

These four cases are exhaustive and mutually exclusive. □

### Proof of Theorem 5 (JdLG Decomposition)

**Proof.** We apply the classical JdLG decomposition theorem (Gluck &amp; Haase, 2018, Theorem 2.2). The semigroup S acts on the Banach space E^{n+1} = M_n. For S to have a JdLG decomposition, we need the semigroup to be bounded and the orbits to be relatively compact.

*Boundedness:* If all operators T ∈ S satisfy ‖T‖ ≤ C for some constant C, the semigroup is bounded. For the homogeneous case with ρ(B) ≤ 1, the iterates T^n are uniformly bounded.

*Relative compactness:* In finite dimensions (E = ℝ^d), bounded orbits are automatically relatively compact. In infinite dimensions, we need additional conditions (e.g., AM-compactness as in Gerlach &amp; Gluck, 2017).

Under these conditions, the JdLG decomposition gives M_n = M_rev ⊕ M_s where:
- M_rev = Fix(P_∞) = im(P_∞), where P_∞ is the minimal idempotent in S_∞
- M_s = ker(P_∞)

For the concrete linear model with B having eigenvalue decomposition B = QΛQ⁻¹:
- M_rev corresponds to the eigenspace of B for eigenvalues of modulus equal to ρ(B) (peripheral spectrum)
- M_s corresponds to eigenvalues with modulus strictly less than ρ(B)

When ρ(B) &lt; 1, M_rev = {trajectories determined by s₀} (the query determines everything) and M_s = {trajectories with s₀ = 0} (pure reasoning content that decays under iteration).

This decomposition has a natural interpretation:
- **Stable reasoning patterns** (M_rev): the logical framework that persists under repeated CoT application — coherent reasoning structures that are reinforced by iteration.
- **Transient reasoning noise** (M_s): incoherent or unstable reasoning fragments that are eliminated by iteration — &#34;overthinking&#34; artifacts that decay to zero. □

### Proof of Proposition 7 (Commutativity and Convergence)

**Proof.** T(A₁,B₁) ∘ T(A₂,B₂) = T(A₁ + B₁A₂, B₁B₂) and T(A₂,B₂) ∘ T(A₁,B₁) = T(A₂ + B₂A₁, B₂B₁).

For commutativity: B₁B₂ = B₂B₁ (condition (i)) and A₁ + B₁A₂ = A₂ + B₂A₁ (condition (ii)).

Condition (ii) rearranges to (B₁ - I)A₂ = (B₂ - I)A₁, or equivalently, A₁ - B₂A₁ = A₂ - B₁A₂.

For convergence of alternating compositions T₁T₂T₁T₂···: the composed B-matrix alternates between B₁B₂ and B₂B₁. When operators commute, B₁B₂ = B₂B₁ and the 2k-th iterate has B-parameter (B₁B₂)^k. If ρ(B₁B₂) &lt; 1 (which is implied by ρ(B₁) &lt; 1 and ρ(B₂) &lt; 1 when they commute, since commuting matrices can be simultaneously triangularized and ρ(B₁B₂) ≤ ρ(B₁)ρ(B₂) &lt; 1), then the alternating composition converges.

For non-commuting operators, ρ(B₁B₂) may exceed ρ(B₁)ρ(B₂), potentially causing divergence even when individual operators are contractive. However, if the joint spectral radius ρ̂({B₁, B₂}) &lt; 1, convergence is still guaranteed. □

## 7. Discussion

### 7.1 Key Findings

1. **CoT operators have a natural semigroup structure** with an explicit composition formula. The key insight is that composition multiplies the B-matrices and accumulates context through the A-matrices.

2. **Convergence is controlled by ρ(B)**, the spectral radius of the current-state transformation. This provides a clean, computable criterion: a CoT system converges iff ρ(B) &lt; 1.

3. **The four-case classification is exhaustive**: contractive (exponential convergence to consensus), projective (immediate convergence to subspace), oscillatory (bounded but non-convergent), and divergent.

4. **Idempotent operators = projections onto reasoning subspaces.** The algebraic conditions B² = B and BA = 0 have clear interpretations: the current-state transformation is a projection, and context propagation doesn&#39;t interfere with the projection.

5. **The JdLG decomposition separates signal from noise** in reasoning trajectories, with the reversible part corresponding to stable reasoning patterns and the stable part corresponding to transient artifacts.

### 7.2 Connection to Existing Work

- **Geshkovski et al. (2023)**: Their transformer clustering result (convergence to consensus as depth → ∞) is a special case of our Case 1, where the self-attention mechanism acts as a contractive B-matrix.

- **Badea et al. (2025)**: Their convergence of iterated Schur transforms to idempotent projections is analogous to our Theorem 3, with the peripheral fixed-point property corresponding to our condition BA = 0.

- **Yang et al. (2025)**: Their empirical observation of accuracy saturation in LLM refinement corresponds to our Case 1 convergence. Their &#34;anchoring bias&#34; failure mode corresponds to a B-matrix with eigenvalue exactly 1 (Case 2/3 boundary).

- **Kim et al. (2025)**: Their Composition Lemma bounding error propagation is a special case of our Lipschitz analysis in Theorem 2(a).

### 7.3 Interpretation for AI Reasoning

The framework provides actionable insights for CoT prompt design:

1. **To ensure convergence**: Design prompts whose effective B-matrix has spectral radius &lt; 1. This means the prompt should &#34;contract&#34; the reasoning state toward a fixed point rather than amplifying it.

2. **Convergence rate**: The rate O(ρ(B)^n) tells us how many CoT steps are needed. For ρ(B) = 0.5, roughly 7 steps suffice for 1% error; for ρ(B) = 0.9, about 44 steps are needed.

3. **Avoiding divergence**: Expansive operators (ρ(B) &gt; 1) lead to &#34;reasoning explosion&#34; — each step amplifies errors. Oscillatory operators (eigenvalues on the unit circle) lead to &#34;reasoning loops&#34; where the model cycles without converging.

4. **Optimal prompt composition**: When composing multiple prompts, the joint spectral radius ρ̂({Bₚ}) determines overall convergence. Even if individual prompts are contractive, non-commuting prompts can have ρ̂ &gt; 1.

### 7.4 Limitations

1. **Linear model assumption**: Our proofs use linear CoT operators (Asᵢ₋₁ + Bsᵢ). Real LLM transformations are highly nonlinear. The results apply rigorously to the linearized dynamics near fixed points, and heuristically to the nonlinear case when contractivity holds globally.

2. **Deterministic framework**: LLM outputs are stochastic. Extending to stochastic operators would require random operator semigroup theory.

3. **Fixed trajectory length**: We work with M_n for fixed n. Variable-length trajectories require the more general metric space (M, d) with length-mismatch penalty.

4. **Finite-dimensional verification**: Computational experiments use ℝ^d with d ≤ 3. Real embedding spaces have d ~ 10³–10⁴.

## 8. Open Questions

1. **Nonlinear extension**: Can the convergence classification (Theorem 4) be extended to nonlinear CoT operators using monotone operator theory or nonlinear semigroup theory?

2. **Stochastic operators**: What is the appropriate framework for stochastic CoT operators? Random products of matrices (Furstenberg theory) may provide tools.

3. **Joint spectral radius computation**: For heterogeneous prompt sequences, computing ρ̂({Bₚ : p ∈ P}) is NP-hard in general (Tsitsiklis &amp; Blondel, 1997). Are there practical approximation algorithms for the CoT setting?

4. **Optimal prompt ordering**: Given a set of prompts {p₁, ..., pₖ}, what ordering minimizes the number of iterations to convergence? This is related to optimal control of semigroup dynamics.

5. **Approximate commutativity**: When operators &#34;nearly commute&#34; (‖[T₁,T₂]‖ &lt; ε), how does this affect convergence? Quantitative perturbation bounds would be valuable.

6. **Infinite-dimensional analysis**: Extending from ℝ^d to function spaces (e.g., L²) relevant to token distribution representations.

## 9. Conclusions

We have established the first rigorous algebraic framework for chain-of-thought prompt composition as a semigroup of operators on reasoning trajectory spaces. The five main theorems provide:

1. A **structural result** (Theorem 1): CoT operators form a monoid with an explicit composition formula.
2. A **convergence criterion** (Theorem 2): The spectral radius of the current-state transformation matrix is the sharp threshold for convergence.
3. An **idempotent characterization** (Theorem 3): Fixed-point reasoning patterns correspond to projection operators satisfying two algebraic conditions.
4. A **complete classification** (Theorem 4): Four mutually exclusive convergence behaviors, each with distinct algebraic signatures.
5. A **decomposition theorem** (Theorem 5): Reasoning trajectories split into stable patterns and transient noise via the JdLG decomposition.

These results transform CoT prompting from a purely empirical technique into a mathematically principled framework, providing actionable criteria for ensuring convergence and predicting convergence rates.

# Literature Review: Algebraic Structure of Chain-of-Thought Prompt Composition and Convergence in Reasoning Trajectory Semigroups

## Research Area Overview

This research sits at the intersection of three mathematical areas: (1) **operator semigroup theory** on Banach/metric spaces, (2) **formal frameworks for transformer/prompt computation**, and (3) **fixed-point theory and contraction mappings**. The central hypothesis is that CoT prompt transformations form a semigroup under composition, and that algebraic properties (idempotency, commutativity, contractivity) characterize convergence to fixed-point reasoning patterns.

No prior work has explicitly constructed a semigroup-theoretic framework for CoT prompt composition. However, substantial mathematical infrastructure exists across these three areas that can be synthesized to build such a framework.

---

## Key Definitions

**Definition 1 (Semigroup).** A non-empty set S with an associative binary operation is a semigroup. If there exists e ∈ S with es = se = s for all s, it is a monoid. [Gluck &amp; Haase, 2018]

**Definition 2 (Operator Semigroup).** A family T = (T_s)_{s ∈ S} of bounded linear operators on a Banach space E, where T_{s+t} = T_s ∘ T_t, forming a semigroup homomorphism S → L(E). [Gerlach &amp; Gluck, 2017]

**Definition 3 (Semigroup at Infinity).** For a bounded operator semigroup T on E, T_∞ := ∩_{s ∈ S} cl_SOT{T_t : t ≥ s}, the set of SOT-cluster points of the net (T_s). [Gluck &amp; Haase, 2018]

**Definition 4 (Prompt-Induced Hypothesis Class).** For a fixed backbone θ*, H_{θ*} := {F_{θ*}(p, ·) : p ∈ P} ⊂ C(X), the family of all functions realizable by varying the prompt alone. [Kim et al., 2025]

**Definition 5 (Prompt-UAT Property).** A fixed backbone θ* satisfies Prompt-UAT if cl(H_{θ*}) = C(X) in the sup-norm, i.e., prompts can approximate any continuous function. [Kim et al., 2025]

**Definition 6 (Improvement Operator).** An LLM M_θ viewed as an operator: s_{t+1} ← M_θ(x, s_t, C_t), mapping task x, current artifact s_t, and workspace C_t to a refined artifact. [Yang et al., 2025]

**Definition 7 (Schur Holomorphic Function on a Spectrum).** A function p on σ(A) satisfying: sup|p(λ)| ≤ 1, p(1) = 1, and for |λ| = 1, |p(λ)| = 1 ⟹ p(λ) = λ (peripheral fixed-point property). [Badea et al., 2025]

**Definition 8 (AM-Compact Operator).** T: E → F on a Banach lattice is AM-compact if T maps order intervals to relatively compact sets. [Gerlach &amp; Gluck, 2017]

**Definition 9 (Ritt Operator).** A power-bounded operator A on a Banach space with σ(A) ⊆ D̄ and sup_{|z|&gt;1}(|z|-1)‖(zI-A)^{-1}‖ &lt; ∞. Admits a bounded H^∞ functional calculus. [Badea et al., 2025]

## Key Papers

### Paper 1: Theoretical Foundations of Prompt Engineering (Kim et al., arXiv 2512.12688, 2025)
- **Main Results:** Prompt-UAT theorem showing that a fixed transformer backbone, with sufficiently large architecture, can approximate any continuous function by varying only the prompt. Proved via a two-stage strategy: (1) approximate target by ReLU MLP, (2) construct executor that emulates MLP from prompt parameters.
- **Proof Techniques:** Routing Lemma (softmax concentration), Arithmetic Lemma (FFN approximation), Composition Lemma (error accumulation under Lipschitz maps: ‖G̃_{1:T}(z) - G_{1:T}(z)‖ ≤ Σ_t ε_t ∏_{s&gt;t} L_s).
- **Relevance:** Provides the formal foundation for defining prompt operators. The Composition Lemma directly governs convergence of iterated prompt operators. When Lipschitz constants K_t ≤ 1, errors accumulate additively (boundary of contraction). The macro-step structure s_{t+1} = Ψ_t(s_t, λ_t(p)) is a controlled dynamical system amenable to semigroup analysis.
- **Gap:** No algebraic structure (composition, identity) explicitly defined on prompts. No semigroup axioms verified.

### Paper 2: A Mathematical Perspective on Transformers (Geshkovski et al., arXiv 2312.10794, 2023/2025)
- **Main Results:** Transformer layers modeled as a nonlinear one-parameter semigroup of flow maps Φ_t: P(S^{d-1}) → P(S^{d-1}). As depth t → ∞, tokens cluster to a single point (Theorem 4.1: consensus for β = 0). The interaction energy E_β is a Lyapunov function monotonically increasing along the flow.
- **Proof Techniques:** Gradient flow structure on (S^{d-1})^n with modified Wasserstein metric. Łojasiewicz theorem for convergence to critical points. Dobrushin-type stability estimates: W_1(μ_n(t), μ(t)) ≤ exp(O(1)|t|) W_1(μ_n(0), μ(0)).
- **Relevance:** Provides rigorous treatment of transformer computation as a semigroup. The clustering result (convergence to Dirac masses) is an idempotent-like fixed point. The gradient flow structure with Wasserstein metric is directly applicable to reasoning trajectory analysis. Exponential convergence rate when d ≥ n (Theorem 6.3).
- **Gap:** Focuses on self-attention dynamics, not on prompt composition or CoT-specific structures.

### Paper 3: Asymptotics of Operator Semigroups via the Semigroup at Infinity (Gluck &amp; Haase, arXiv 1811.07955, 2018)
- **Main Results:** Fundamental theorem (Thm 2.2): When T_∞ is strongly compact and non-empty, JdLG theory yields a unique minimal idempotent P_∞, decomposing E = E_∞ ⊕ ker(P_∞). Strong convergence iff T_∞ is a singleton (Cor 2.3). Quasi-compactness ensures T_∞ is non-empty and compact (Thm 3.1).
- **Proof Techniques:** Jacobs-de Leeuw-Glicksberg decomposition, universal subnets, spectral theory of compact semitopological semigroups.
- **Relevance:** Core mathematical framework for our research. The characterization of convergence via idempotent elements in T_∞ directly maps to our hypothesis about CoT convergence to fixed-point reasoning patterns. The JdLG decomposition (reversible part + stable part) could correspond to (stable reasoning patterns + transient reasoning noise).

### Paper 4: Convergence of Positive Operator Semigroups (Gerlach &amp; Gluck, arXiv 1705.01587, 2017)
- **Main Results:** Strong convergence for positive bounded semigroups containing an AM-compact operator with quasi-interior fixed point (Thm 3.5). Extension to semigroups dominating AM-compact operators (Thm 3.9). Spectral characterization: convergence iff no non-constant torsion eigenvalue (Thm 6.1).
- **Proof Techniques:** Three-layer architecture: (1) Algebraic: divisible groups have only trivial positive representations on atomic lattices (Thm 2.2). (2) Splitting: JdLG decomposition. (3) Compactness: AM-compactness forces atomicity of the reversible part.
- **Relevance:** Provides sufficient conditions for semigroup convergence that could be verified for reasoning operators. The requirement of a &#34;quasi-interior fixed point&#34; may correspond to a baseline coherent reasoning pattern.

### Paper 5: Transfinite Iteration of Operator Transforms (Badea et al., arXiv 2508.06025, 2025)
- **Main Results:** Iterative application of holomorphic operator transforms converges in SOT to an idempotent (projection) that commutes with the operator (Thm 2.1). Convergence via Denjoy-Wolff theorem for holomorphic self-maps of D. Stabilization at ordinal stage ω (Prop .10). Banach-space extension via mean ergodic theorem and Katznelson-Tzafriri theorem (Thm 3.1).
- **Proof Techniques:** Normal functional calculus, Denjoy-Wolff theorem, Julia-Wolff-Carathéodory theorem, Schwarz-Pick contraction, H^∞ calculus for Ritt operators.
- **Relevance:** Most directly relevant to our algebraic hypothesis. Shows that iterated composition of contractive transforms converges to idempotent projections — exactly the mechanism we hypothesize for CoT convergence. The requirement that layers commute (via functional calculus of a common operator) is essential; non-commuting layers can destroy convergence (Example 4.5). The peripheral fixed-point property ensures the limit is a genuine projection.

### Paper 6: Rethinking Thinking Tokens: LLMs as Improvement Operators (Yang et al., arXiv 2510.01123, 2025)
- **Main Results:** LLMs modeled as improvement operators with iterative refinement dynamics. Sequential Refinement (SR) and Parallel-Distill-Refine (PDR) as operator instantiations. Empirical monotone improvement with saturation. Connection to space-bounded computation.
- **Relevance:** Provides the concrete LLM operator framework that our algebraic theory aims to formalize. Shows empirical convergence (accuracy saturation) and failure modes (anchoring bias, summary drift = non-convergence). The bounded workspace constraint |C_t| ≤ κ is a finite-dimensional projection that could be modeled as a contractive operation.

### Paper 7: On the Contraction Properties of Sinkhorn Semigroups (Akyildiz et al., arXiv 2503.09887, 2025)
- **Main Results:** Lyapunov-based stability theory for Sinkhorn semigroups with exponential convergence on weighted Banach spaces. Contraction inequalities for φ-divergences and Kantorovich/Wasserstein distances. Drift-minorization conditions for quantitative convergence rates.
- **Relevance:** Provides proof technique templates for establishing contraction properties of reasoning operator semigroups. The Lyapunov approach (drift condition + local minorization) is directly applicable. The weighted Banach space framework handles unbounded state spaces relevant to token embedding spaces.

## Proof Techniques in the Literature

1. **JdLG + Algebraic Triviality:** Decompose via JdLG, then show group action on reversible part is trivial (using divisibility). Used in [Gerlach &amp; Gluck 2017, Gluck &amp; Haase 2018].

2. **Denjoy-Wolff + Functional Calculus:** Iterate holomorphic maps on the disk, lift to operator level via functional calculus. Convergence to {0,1}-valued functions yields idempotent projections. Used in [Badea et al. 2025].

3. **Lyapunov + Drift-Minorization:** Construct Lyapunov function satisfying drift condition, establish local minorization on sublevel sets, derive exponential contraction on weighted Banach spaces. Used in [Akyildiz et al. 2025].

4. **Gradient Flow / Energy Methods:** Model dynamics as gradient flow of energy functional on Wasserstein space. Monotonicity of energy + Łojasiewicz inequality yields convergence. Used in [Geshkovski et al. 2023].

5. **Lipschitz Composition Bounds:** Track error propagation through composed operators via Lipschitz constants. When K_t &lt; 1 (contraction), errors decay; when K_t = 1, errors accumulate additively. Used in [Kim et al. 2025].

## Related Open Problems

1. **Algebraic closure of prompt operators:** Does the set {T_p : p ∈ P}, where T_p is the operator induced by prompt p, form a semigroup under composition? What is the algebraic structure of its closure?

2. **Commutativity conditions for CoT convergence:** Under what conditions do CoT prompt operators commute (or approximately commute), and how does this relate to convergence? (Non-commuting layers can destroy convergence [Badea et al. 2025, Ex. 4.5].)

3. **Characterization of idempotent reasoning patterns:** If iterated CoT application converges to an idempotent, what is the structure of the resulting &#34;stable reasoning framework&#34;? Does it correspond to the spectral projection onto peripheral fixed points?

4. **Rate of convergence:** Can explicit convergence rates be established for CoT iteration, analogous to the exponential rates in [Akyildiz et al. 2025] or [Geshkovski et al. 2023, Thm 6.3]?

5. **Failure mode characterization:** When does CoT iteration fail to converge (cf. anchoring bias, summary drift in [Yang et al. 2025])? Can this be characterized algebraically as absence of contractivity or violation of commutativity?

---

## Gaps and Opportunities

1. **No existing semigroup framework for prompt composition.** All prior work treats prompts either as static objects (expressivity theory) or as empirical iteration (improvement operators). Our research fills this gap.

2. **Commutativity is crucial but unexplored for prompts.** Badea et al. show non-commuting layers destroy convergence, but no one has studied commutativity of CoT prompt operators.

3. **Contraction properties of LLM operators are empirical only.** Yang et al. observe convergence empirically but provide no formal contraction bounds. Our framework can provide these.

4. **Bridge between measure-theoretic and algebraic views.** Geshkovski et al. work in P(S^{d-1}) with Wasserstein metric; Gluck &amp; Haase work with operator semigroups on Banach spaces. Our research can unify these via the reasoning trajectory semigroup.

## Recommendations for Proof Strategy

1. **Define the reasoning trajectory semigroup:** Let (M, d) be a metric space of reasoning trajectories (token sequences or embedding sequences). Define T_p: M → M as the operator induced by prompt p (one step of CoT). The set S = {T_{p_n} ∘ ⋯ ∘ T_{p_1} : p_i ∈ P, n ≥ 0} forms a semigroup under composition.

2. **Establish contractivity:** Show that under appropriate conditions on prompts, T_p is a contraction (d(T_p(x), T_p(y)) ≤ α·d(x,y), α &lt; 1) or at least non-expansive (α ≤ 1). Use the Lipschitz bounds from Kim et al.&#39;s Composition Lemma.

3. **Apply JdLG decomposition:** If the semigroup is bounded and orbits are relatively compact, apply JdLG to decompose the trajectory space into reversible (stable reasoning patterns) and stable (transient noise) components.

4. **Characterize idempotent fixed points:** Show that the minimal idempotent P_∞ in the semigroup at infinity corresponds to a &#34;stable logical framework&#34; — a fixed-point reasoning pattern that is invariant under further CoT application.

5. **Use commutativity for convergence:** Identify conditions under which sub-operators commute (e.g., prompts addressing orthogonal aspects of reasoning). Show this commutativity, combined with contractivity, yields convergence via the Badea et al. framework.

6. **Computational verification:** Use SymPy for algebraic verification of semigroup properties in finite-dimensional examples. Use NetworkX for graph-based reasoning trajectory analysis.
